How Networks Work

VCDTDS035 — Data Transmission & Security in Networks

🌐 What is a Network?

A network is a group of connected devices that can share information with each other. When you stream a video, send a message, or load a website, your data travels through a network.

There are three main types of networks — click each one to explore its pros, cons, and best uses!

🔌
Wired (Ethernet)
Tap to explore ↓

Devices are connected using physical Ethernet cables plugged directly into a router or switch. Data travels through copper or fibre optic cables at very high speeds.

✅ Pros
  • Fastest speeds
  • Most reliable — no dropouts
  • Very secure — hard to intercept
  • Low latency (great for gaming)
❌ Cons
  • Can't move around freely
  • Cables can be messy
  • Expensive to install in large spaces
  • Not practical for phones/tablets
🏆 Best used for

School computer labs, office workstations, gaming setups, bank terminals, hospital records systems — anywhere that needs speed, security and reliability.

📶
Wi-Fi (Wireless)
Tap to explore ↓

Data is sent through radio waves from a wireless router. Devices connect without cables within a certain range (usually up to ~50 metres indoors).

✅ Pros
  • No cables needed
  • Multiple devices connect easily
  • Move freely within range
  • Easy to set up
❌ Cons
  • Slower than wired
  • Signal drops through walls
  • Can be hacked if unsecured
  • Congested with many users
🏆 Best used for

Homes, cafés, schools, libraries, shopping centres — anywhere people need convenient internet access and don't need maximum speed or security.

📱
Mobile (4G/5G)
Tap to explore ↓

Data travels via mobile towers (cell towers) that blanket a wide area. Your phone connects to the nearest tower, which connects to the internet. 5G is the newest and fastest generation.

✅ Pros
  • Works almost anywhere
  • No router needed
  • Great for on-the-go use
  • 5G is extremely fast
❌ Cons
  • Costs money (data plans)
  • Dead zones in rural areas
  • Can slow down in crowds
  • Uses more battery
🏆 Best used for

Smartphones, emergency services in the field, remote workers, GPS navigation, delivery drivers, hikers — anyone who needs internet access while moving around outside.

📦 How Data Actually Travels — Packets!

When you send data (like a photo), it doesn't travel as one big chunk. It gets broken into tiny pieces called packetsSmall chunks of data sent individually through a network.

Each packet contains: the data itself, where it's going (destination address), where it came from (source address), and a packet number so it can be reassembled correctly at the other end.

Packets can travel different routes through the internet and are reassembled at the destination — like pieces of a puzzle!

Data, Analysis & Visualisation

VCDTDI038 — Analyse & Visualise Data

📊 What is Data Visualisation?

Raw data (like a spreadsheet full of numbers) is hard for humans to understand at a glance. Data visualisation turns data into charts, graphs, and diagrams that make patterns easy to spot.

Good data analysts ask: "What story does this data tell?" — and choose the right chart type to answer it. Explore the four most common types below!

📊 Bar / Column Chart
Tap to learn more ↓

Uses rectangular bars to compare values across different categories. Taller bar = bigger value. Easy to read at a glance.

✅ Use when

Comparing things side by side — e.g. most popular sports, sales by month, votes per candidate.

❌ Avoid when

Showing change over time (use a line chart) or parts of a whole (use a pie chart).

📈 Line Chart
Tap to learn more ↓

Connects data points with a line to show how a value changes over time. Rising line = increasing trend. Falling line = decreasing trend.

✅ Use when

Tracking something over time — e.g. temperature across a week, website visits over months, your exam scores each term.

❌ Avoid when

Data has no time order — e.g. comparing favourite colours in a class (use a bar chart instead).

🥧 Pie Chart
Tap to learn more ↓

A circle divided into slices — each slice represents a proportion of the whole. All slices always add up to 100%.

✅ Use when

Showing how something is divided up — e.g. percentage of students using each device, market share of phone brands, budget breakdown.

❌ Avoid when

You have many categories (hard to read with 6+ slices) or need to show precise differences — small slices are difficult to compare.

✦ Scatter Plot
Tap to learn more ↓

Plots individual data points on two axes to reveal relationships between two variables. A pattern of dots trending up-right means a positive correlation.

✅ Use when

Investigating if two things are linked — e.g. does more sleep = better grades? Does temperature affect ice cream sales?

❌ Avoid when

You only have one variable or categorical data — a scatter plot needs two numerical values per data point.

🗂️ Structured Data — Modelling Real Things

Computers use structured data to model real-world objects and events. Every row represents one real thing (like a student, a product, or a weather reading) and every column stores one type of information about it.

This structure lets computers sort, filter, search and compare data instantly — imagine trying to find the highest scorer in a class of 500 by reading through a paragraph about each student vs. scanning a column of numbers!

A well-structured dataset has: consistent column names, one data type per column, and no missing values.

Designing Algorithms

VCDTCD042 — Algorithms: Diagrammatic & English Design

🔷 What is an Algorithm?

An algorithm is a step-by-step set of instructions to solve a problem. Algorithms are the recipe that programmers turn into code.

Before writing any code, good programmers design their algorithm in plain language or as a flowchart. This helps find errors early!

🗺️ Why Use a Flowchart?

Imagine trying to explain a complicated game to a friend using only words — it gets confusing fast. A flowchart turns your algorithm into a visual diagram, making it much easier to understand, check, and share.

🐛 Catch mistakes early

Drawing the steps out reveals logic errors before you write a single line of code — much easier to fix a diagram than broken code!

🤝 Share your thinking

A flowchart is a universal language — another programmer anywhere in the world can read your diagram and understand exactly how your program works.

🧩 Break big problems down

Complex programs can have hundreds of steps. A flowchart lets you map out each decision and action clearly, so nothing gets missed.

📐 Why Do Flowcharts Use Specific Symbols?

Just like road signs have standard shapes (a triangle means warning, a circle means instruction), flowcharts use agreed symbols so that everyone reads them the same way. These symbols are used by programmers, engineers and designers worldwide.

⬭
Oval — Terminator
Marks the START or END of the algorithm. Every flowchart must have both.
▭
Rectangle — Process
An action the computer performs internally, e.g. "Add 1 to score" or "Sort the list".
◇
Diamond — Decision
A yes/no question that creates a branch, e.g. "Is score > 50?". Always has two exits: YES and NO.
▱
Parallelogram — Input / Output
Data coming in (user types something) or going out (displaying a result on screen).
→
Arrow — Flow
Connects shapes and shows the direction the algorithm moves — always follow the arrows!
💡 Think of it like this: the shape tells you what type of step it is, and the text inside tells you what specifically happens. Together they make the diagram self-explanatory to anyone who reads it.

Evaluate & Improve Solutions

VCDTCD044 — Evaluate Solutions: Needs, Innovation & Sustainability

🔍 Why Do We Evaluate?

Building something is only half the job. A good digital technologist asks: "Does this actually solve the problem? Could it be better? Will it still work in 5 years?"

When evaluating a digital solution, we look at three key areas:

  • Meeting needs — Does it actually do what users need?
  • Innovation — Is it a creative or clever approach?
  • Sustainability — Does it consider economic, environmental, and social impacts?

♻️ The Three Types of Sustainability

  • 🌱 Environmental — Does it use less energy? Does it reduce waste? (e.g. paperless systems)
  • 💰 Economic — Is it affordable to run long-term? Does it create or destroy jobs?
  • 🤝 Social — Is it accessible to everyone? Does it respect privacy and human rights?
