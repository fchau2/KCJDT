📡 Module 1 — How Networks Work

🌐 What is a Network?

A network is any group of devices connected so they can share information. The internet is the world's largest network. When a student streams a video or sends a message, that data is travelling across a network in real time.

Think of it like a postal system — instead of letters, computers send small bundles of data, and instead of roads, data travels through cables or radio waves.

💡 Classroom hook: Ask students "What devices at home are connected to your network?" (TVs, game consoles, phones, smart speakers — most will be surprised by how many there are.)

🔌 The Three Network Types

Wired (Ethernet) — A physical cable connects devices directly. It's the fastest and most reliable option because the signal can't be interrupted by walls or interference. Used in schools, offices, and gaming setups where speed and stability matter. The cable plugs into a port on the device and into a router or switch.

Wi-Fi (Wireless) — A router converts data into radio waves and broadcasts them in all directions. Any device within range can connect without a cable. Convenient but slightly slower than wired, and affected by distance and physical barriers like walls.

Mobile 4G/5G — Devices connect to the internet through a network of cell towers maintained by telecommunications companies (Telstra, Optus etc.). Used when there's no Wi-Fi available. 5G is the newest generation — it can be faster than home Wi-Fi but coverage varies.

💡 Analogy: Wired = a direct phone call on a landline. Wi-Fi = a cordless phone at home. Mobile = a mobile phone anywhere with signal.

📦 Data Packets

When you send a file or load a website, your data doesn't travel as one large piece. It's broken into small chunks called packets — each packet travels independently across the network and may take a different route. At the destination, they're reassembled in order.

This is efficient because: if one packet gets lost or delayed, only that piece needs to be resent, not the whole file. It also allows many users to share the same network simultaneously.

💡 Analogy: Imagine mailing a 10-page letter by tearing it into 10 separate envelopes and posting them individually. They might arrive in different orders, but you reassemble them at the end.

🔒 Encryption

Encryption is the process of scrambling data so that only the intended recipient can read it. When data travels across a network it passes through many devices — encryption ensures that even if someone intercepts it, it appears as meaningless gibberish without the decryption key.

HTTPS (the padlock icon in browsers) means the connection is encrypted. Online banking, shopping, and school logins all use encryption.

💡 Simple explanation: It's like writing a message in a secret code that only the receiver knows how to decode.
Activities
◈ Activity 1 — Matching Game

Match the Network Type to the Situation

Students match three real-world scenarios (nurse moving around a hospital, students in a school lab with cables, someone watching Netflix at home) to the correct network type. This checks whether students can apply their knowledge rather than just recall definitions. Correct answers: nurse → Mobile, school lab → Wired, Netflix at home → Wi-Fi.

◈ Activity 2 — Quick Questions

Test Your Network Knowledge

Three multiple-choice questions covering: what data packets are, which network is most reliable for gaming (wired ethernet), and what encryption does. Each question has instant feedback. If students are stuck on Q3, remind them that encryption is about security, not speed.

◈ Activity 3 — Drag & Sort

Which Network is Best?

Students drag use-case cards (e.g. "Watching a movie on the bus", "Connecting a desktop computer in a classroom") into the correct network category column. Encourages students to justify their reasoning. There can be edge cases — encourage discussion rather than just right/wrong thinking.

🛡️ Cyber Security Threats

This section introduces five real-world cyber threats that students are likely to encounter or hear about. The goal is not just memorisation — students should be able to recognise each threat in context and explain what defence applies and why.

🦠 Virus — Malicious code that attaches itself to files and spreads when those files are opened or shared. Can corrupt data or slow down devices. Defence: antivirus software and not opening files from unknown sources.

☠️ Malware — A broad category covering any malicious software including spyware, ransomware, and trojans. Often installed without the user's knowledge. Defence: keeping software updated and avoiding suspicious downloads.

📧 Phishing — A social engineering attack where a fake email or website tricks users into giving away passwords or personal information. Often disguised as a trusted organisation (bank, school, government). Defence: spam filters and always checking the sender address carefully.

👾 Hackers — Individuals who try to gain unauthorised access to accounts or systems, often through guessing passwords or exploiting weak security. Defence: strong unique passwords and two-factor authentication (2FA).

💥 DDoS Attack — A Distributed Denial of Service attack floods a server with millions of fake requests simultaneously, overwhelming it and causing it to crash or go offline. Defence: firewalls and rate limiting to block excess traffic.

💡 Real-world hook: Ask students if they've ever received a suspicious email or seen a "your account has been hacked" message. In 2016, a DDoS attack took down Twitter, Netflix, and Spotify across the US for hours. These aren't abstract concepts.
💡 Teaching tip: Students often confuse virus and malware — clarify that a virus is a type of malware. All viruses are malware, but not all malware is a virus (e.g. ransomware isn't a virus).
◈ Activity 4 — Cyber Threats Match

Match Each Threat to Its Best Defence

Students click a cyber threat on the left column, then click the matching defence on the right. There are 4 pairs to match. Correct answers: Virus → Antivirus software & don't open unknown files; Phishing → Spam filter & check sender address; DDoS → Firewall & rate limiting; Hacker → Strong password & two-factor authentication.

Wrong selections produce a shake animation — students can keep trying until all 4 are matched correctly. Progress is tracked individually per pair so the teacher report shows which specific threats a student struggled with.

💡 If students struggle with DDoS, remind them it's about volume — it's not stealing data, it's overwhelming a server with traffic. A firewall blocks suspicious sources; rate limiting caps how many requests any single source can make.
◈ Activity 5 — Cyber Security Quiz

Test Your Cyber Knowledge

Two scenario-based questions that require students to identify attack types from context rather than just recall definitions. Q4 presents a suspicious email from "skoo1.net" (misspelled domain — a classic phishing tell). Q5 describes a server flooded with fake connection requests — a DDoS attack. Both have instant feedback. If students mix them up, discuss the key difference: phishing targets people through deception, DDoS targets servers through volume.

📊 Module 2 — Data, Analysis & Visualisation

📊 What is Data Visualisation?

Raw data (a spreadsheet of numbers) is hard for humans to interpret quickly. Data visualisation converts numbers into charts and graphs that reveal patterns instantly. A good data analyst doesn't just make pretty charts — they choose the right chart for the story the data tells.

💡 Real-world connection: Weather apps, sports statistics, COVID dashboards, and school reports all use data visualisation. Ask students where they've seen charts recently.

📈 The Four Chart Types

Bar Chart — Best for comparing values across categories (e.g. most popular sports, sales by product). Each bar height represents the value. Easy to read at a glance.

Line Chart — Best for showing change over time (e.g. temperature across a week, growth over months). The slope of the line shows the trend — rising means increasing, falling means decreasing.

Pie Chart — Best when showing parts of a whole that add up to 100% (e.g. market share, class survey results). Avoid when there are more than 5–6 categories — too many slices become unreadable.

Scatter Plot — Best for exploring whether two variables are related (e.g. does more study lead to higher scores?). Each dot represents one data point with two measurements. A trend line shows the direction of the relationship.

💡 Common mistake: Students often choose pie charts for everything. Emphasise that pie charts only make sense when the values add up to a meaningful total (100%).

🗂️ Structured Data

Structured data organises information into rows and columns — like a spreadsheet or database table. Each row is one record (e.g. one student), and each column is one type of information (e.g. name, age, score). This structure lets computers sort, filter and search data instantly.

Key quality rules: consistent column names, one data type per column (don't mix numbers and text), and no missing values. Poor data quality leads to incorrect analysis — "garbage in, garbage out."

💡 Classroom activity: Ask students to imagine designing a table to record everyone's favourite subject and score. What columns would they need? What would happen if someone wrote "eighty" instead of "80"?
Activities

Students choose a dataset (class scores, weekly temperature, favourite sports, or study hours vs score) then select a chart type. The chart animates on screen and a verdict explains whether their choice was appropriate — and why. This directly builds the skill of choosing the right chart for the data. The "Study vs Score" dataset is the trickiest — it's the only one where a scatter plot is the clear best choice.

📊 Explore — Interactive Chart

An interactive bar chart showing real-looking survey data. Students can click individual bars to see exact values and think about what the data tells them. Good for prompting discussion: "What does this data suggest about how students prefer to learn? What might we do about it?"

🔍 Activity — Spot the Problems

What's Wrong With This Data Table?

Students click on errors in a deliberately flawed data table (3 mistakes hidden in it). Problems include things like inconsistent formatting, wrong data types, and missing values. After finding all 3, a follow-up collection task unlocks. This reinforces data quality concepts in a hands-on way.

✏️ Create — Data Visualisation

Create Your Own Data Visualisation

Students collect their own data from classmates (e.g. favourite subjects, hours of sleep) and plan a visualisation. This is an open-ended creative task — encourage students to justify their chart choice in writing before drawing it. A good extension: ask them to write one sentence describing the "story" their chart tells.

🔷 Module 3 — Designing Algorithms

🔷 What is an Algorithm?

An algorithm is a precise, step-by-step set of instructions for solving a problem or completing a task. The key word is precise — computers follow instructions exactly as written, so any ambiguity or missing step causes errors.

Algorithms are everywhere: a recipe is an algorithm for cooking; the rules of a board game are an algorithm for playing; a GPS route is an algorithm for navigating.

💡 Classroom demo: Ask a student to give you instructions to make a sandwich, then follow them exactly and literally. They'll quickly discover that "put the bread down" without specifying which side leads to chaos — computers have this same problem.

🗺️ Flowcharts

A flowchart is a visual way to represent an algorithm using shapes connected by arrows. They're useful because they make the logic easy to follow, especially when there are decisions or repeating steps involved. Flowcharts are used by software developers, engineers, and business analysts to plan processes before writing code.

💡 Key point: Flowcharts are not just diagrams — they're a thinking tool. Drawing one forces you to work out every step and decision before you start building.

📐 Flowchart Symbols

Each shape has a specific meaning — this is a universal standard used across the world:

Oval (Terminator) — marks the START or END of the algorithm. Every flowchart must have exactly one start and one end.

Rectangle (Process) — an action or instruction (e.g. "Add 1 to the counter", "Open the app"). This is the most common shape.

Diamond (Decision) — a yes/no question that creates a branch (e.g. "Is the password correct?"). Two arrows leave: one for Yes, one for No.

Parallelogram (Input/Output) — data entering or leaving the system (e.g. "User types their name", "Display the result").

💡 Memory trick: Diamond = decision (both start with D). Rectangle = regular process steps (boring rectangles for ordinary actions).
Activities
🧠 Activity 1 — Symbol ID

Which Shape is Which?

Students are shown flowchart shapes and must identify each one. Multiple-choice format with instant feedback. Good to do as a class first — display the shapes on the board and cold-call students before they attempt it individually. Reinforce that getting the shapes wrong in real flowcharts causes confusion for anyone reading them.

🖱️ Activity 2 — Build a Flowchart

Put the "Searching for a Song" Algorithm in Order

Students drag steps into the correct sequence to build a flowchart for searching for a song in a music app. This includes an input step (typing the name), a process (searching), a decision (was it found?), and two outcomes. The correct order emphasises that you must open the app before you can type, and you must search before you can get a result.

🌿 Activity 3 — Branching Flowchart

Build the Branching Flowchart

A more complex drag-and-build activity where students create a flowchart with a decision diamond that branches into two different paths (e.g. a game login scenario). This introduces the concept that algorithms can take different paths depending on conditions — the foundation of all computer programming logic (if/else statements).

🔁 Activity 4 — Looping Flowchart

Build the Looping Flowchart

Students build a flowchart where the algorithm loops back on itself until a condition is met (e.g. keep guessing until correct). Loops are fundamental to programming — they allow computers to repeat tasks automatically without the programmer writing the same instruction many times. This is often the concept students find trickiest; use the analogy of snoozing an alarm repeatedly until you actually get up.

🔍 Activity 5 — Trace the Algorithm

Find the Bug!

Students are shown a flowchart with a logical error (a "bug") and must trace through the steps to identify where the algorithm produces the wrong result. This develops computational thinking — specifically the skill of tracing and debugging. Emphasise that finding bugs is a normal and important part of all programming and engineering work.

✏️ Design — Your Own Flowchart

Design Your Own Flowchart

Open-ended task where students choose their own everyday process and map it as a flowchart using correct symbols. Good topics: making breakfast, deciding what to wear, choosing a Netflix show. Look for: correct symbol use, at least one decision diamond with two branches, a clear START and END. A common mistake is forgetting the terminator ovals.

🔍 Module 4 — Evaluate & Improve Solutions

🔍 Why Do We Evaluate?

Evaluation means assessing how well a digital solution meets its intended purpose. In the real world, software is never "finished" — it's continuously evaluated and improved based on feedback from users, changes in technology, and new needs. Good designers ask: does it actually solve the problem it was meant to? Does it work for the people using it?

💡 Real-world example: Instagram, YouTube, and apps students use daily are updated constantly based on user feedback and evaluation. Ask students: "What would you improve about an app you use?"

👥 Meeting User Needs

A solution must work for its intended users. This means considering: Is it easy to use? Is the language appropriate? Does it work on the devices users have? Does it accommodate different abilities (accessibility)?

A technically impressive solution that confuses its users is not a good solution. User testing — watching real users try to use the product — is how developers find these issues.

💡 Innovation

Innovation means introducing something new or improved that adds value. In digital technology, this might mean a feature that didn't exist before, a faster way of doing something, or a better user experience. Innovation doesn't have to be groundbreaking — small improvements that genuinely help users count too.

♻️ The Three Types of Sustainability

Environmental sustainability — Does the solution minimise energy use and e-waste? Data centres use enormous amounts of electricity. Efficient code and hardware choices matter.

Economic sustainability — Can users afford it? Is the business model viable long-term? Free apps often have hidden costs (advertising, data collection).

Social sustainability — Does the solution benefit society fairly? Does it exclude any groups? Could it cause harm (e.g. addiction by design, misinformation)?

💡 Discussion prompt: "Is TikTok sustainable? Think about all three types." This often generates excellent debate.
Activities
📚 Activity 1 — Case Study

Evaluate This App! (FarmSmart)

Students evaluate a fictional farming app called FarmSmart using the concepts from the module. They identify strengths (what it does well for users), weaknesses (what could be improved), and consider sustainability. This is a structured evaluation using a real-world scenario — agriculture technology is a growing field in Australia, making it locally relevant.

🖱️ Activity 2 — Sort It Out

Is This a Strength or a Weakness?

Students drag evaluation statements into "strength" or "weakness" categories. Statements are deliberately nuanced — some things that sound positive could be weaknesses depending on context. Encourage students to explain their reasoning rather than just sorting quickly. This builds critical thinking alongside digital literacy.

📋 Reference — Evaluation Rubric

Use This to Evaluate ANY Digital Solution

A structured rubric with criteria across multiple dimensions (functionality, usability, sustainability, innovation). Students can use this as a reference for the open-ended task. It's designed to be applicable to any digital product — not just apps. Encourage students to use specific evidence when applying each criterion rather than vague statements like "it's good."

✏️ Evaluate — Your Choice

Evaluate a Real App You Use Every Day

Open-ended task where students apply the evaluation rubric to an app of their own choosing. This is the highest-order task in the module — it requires synthesis of all the module's concepts. Look for: specific examples as evidence, consideration of multiple stakeholders (not just the student's own experience), and at least one improvement suggestion with reasoning.

VCDTDS035 — Data Transmission & Security in Networks

What is a Network?

A network is a group of connected devices that can share information with each other. When you stream a video, send a message, or load a website, your data travels through a network.

There are three main types of networks — click each one to explore its pros, cons, and best uses!

Wired (Ethernet)
Tap to explore ↓

Devices are connected using physical Ethernet cables plugged directly into a router or switch. Data travels through copper or fibre optic cables at very high speeds.

✓ Pros
  • Fastest speeds
  • Most reliable — no dropouts
  • Very secure — hard to intercept
  • Low latency (great for gaming)
✕ Cons
  • Can't move around freely
  • Cables can be messy
  • Expensive to install in large spaces
  • Not practical for phones/tablets
★ Best used for

School computer labs, office workstations, gaming setups, bank terminals, hospital records systems — anywhere that needs speed, security and reliability.

Wi-Fi (Wireless)
Tap to explore ↓

Data is sent through radio waves from a wireless router. Devices connect without cables within a certain range (usually up to ~50 metres indoors).

✓ Pros
  • No cables needed
  • Multiple devices connect easily
  • Move freely within range
  • Easy to set up
✕ Cons
  • Slower than wired
  • Signal drops through walls
  • Can be hacked if unsecured
  • Congested with many users
★ Best used for

Homes, cafés, schools, libraries, shopping centres — anywhere people need convenient internet access and don't need maximum speed or security.

Mobile (4G/5G)
Tap to explore ↓

Data travels via mobile towers (cell towers) that blanket a wide area. Your phone connects to the nearest tower, which connects to the internet. 5G is the newest and fastest generation.

✓ Pros
  • Works almost anywhere
  • No router needed
  • Great for on-the-go use
  • 5G is extremely fast
✕ Cons
  • Costs money (data plans)
  • Dead zones in rural areas
  • Can slow down in crowds
  • Uses more battery
★ Best used for

Smartphones, emergency services in the field, remote workers, GPS navigation, delivery drivers, hikers — anyone who needs internet access while moving around outside.

How Data Actually Travels — Packets!

When you send data (like a photo), it doesn't travel as one big chunk. It gets broken into tiny pieces called packetsSmall chunks of data sent individually through a network.

Each packet contains: the data itself, where it's going (destination address), where it came from (source address), and a packet number so it can be reassembled correctly at the other end.

Packets can travel different routes through the internet and are reassembled at the destination — like pieces of a puzzle!

Cyber Threats & How to Stay Safe

Networks are incredibly useful — but they can also be targeted by cyber threats. Understanding these threats is the first step to defending against them. Tap each threat to learn what it does and how to stop it.

🦠
Virus
Tap to explore ↓

A computer virus is a piece of harmful code that secretly copies itself and spreads to other files or devices. Like a biological virus, it needs a host — usually a file you download or open.

⚠️ What it does
  • Corrupts or deletes files
  • Slows your device down
  • Spreads to USB drives & emails
🛡️ Defence
  • Install antivirus software
  • Don't open unknown files
  • Keep software updated

Real example: The ILOVEYOU virus (2000) spread via email, tricked millions of people into opening it, and deleted files worldwide.

☠️
Malware
Tap to explore ↓

Malware (short for malicious software) is an umbrella term for any software designed to harm. Viruses are one type — but malware also includes spyware, ransomware, and trojans.

⚠️ What it does
  • Spies on what you type
  • Locks your files for ransom
  • Steals passwords & data
🛡️ Defence
  • Anti-malware software
  • Only download trusted apps
  • Regular system scans

Real example: Ransomware called WannaCry (2017) locked files on hundreds of thousands of computers and demanded payment to unlock them.

📧
Spam & Phishing
Tap to explore ↓

Spam is unwanted junk email sent to millions of people. Phishing is a more dangerous version — fake emails pretending to be from banks, schools, or companies to trick you into handing over your password or clicking a bad link.

⚠️ What it does
  • Tricks you into fake websites
  • Steals login details
  • Spreads viruses via links
🛡️ Defence
  • Spam filters on email
  • Never click unknown links
  • Check the sender address

Spot a phishing email: Urgent language ("Act NOW!"), misspelled sender address, links that don't match the company name.

👾
Hackers
Tap to explore ↓

A hacker is someone who tries to gain unauthorised access to a computer system or network. Some hackers steal data, others cause damage, and some are hired by companies to find weaknesses (called ethical hackers or penetration testers).

⚠️ What they do
  • Break into accounts & systems
  • Steal personal or financial data
  • Take control of devices
🛡️ Defence
  • Strong, unique passwords
  • Two-factor authentication
  • Keep software patched

Strong password tip: Use a passphrase — three random words like "purple-rocket-banana" is stronger than "P@ssw0rd".

💥
DDoS Attack
Tap to explore ↓

A DDoS attack (Distributed Denial of Service) floods a server with so many fake requests that it becomes overwhelmed and crashes — like millions of people all calling the same phone number at once so no real calls can get through.

⚠️ What it does
  • Crashes websites & servers
  • Disrupts online services
  • Causes financial damage
🛡️ Defence
  • Firewalls to filter traffic
  • Rate limiting (block excess)
  • Cloud-based DDoS protection

Real example: In 2016, a DDoS attack took down major websites including Twitter, Netflix, and Spotify for hours across the US.

Data, Analysis & Visualisation

VCDTDI038 — Analyse & Visualise Data

What is Data Visualisation?

Raw data (like a spreadsheet full of numbers) is hard for humans to understand at a glance. Data visualisation turns data into charts, graphs, and diagrams that make patterns easy to spot.

Good data analysts ask: "What story does this data tell?" — and choose the right chart type to answer it. Explore the four most common types below!

Bar / Column Chart
Tap to learn more ↓

Uses rectangular bars to compare values across different categories. Taller bar = bigger value. Easy to read at a glance.

✅ Use when

Comparing things side by side — e.g. most popular sports, sales by month, votes per candidate.

❌ Avoid when

Showing change over time (use a line chart) or parts of a whole (use a pie chart).

Line Chart
Tap to learn more ↓

Connects data points with a line to show how a value changes over time. Rising line = increasing trend. Falling line = decreasing trend.

✅ Use when

Tracking something over time — e.g. temperature across a week, website visits over months, your exam scores each term.

❌ Avoid when

Data has no time order — e.g. comparing favourite colours in a class (use a bar chart instead).

Pie Chart
Tap to learn more ↓

A circle divided into slices — each slice represents a proportion of the whole. All slices always add up to 100%.

✅ Use when

Showing how something is divided up — e.g. percentage of students using each device, market share of phone brands, budget breakdown.

❌ Avoid when

You have many categories (hard to read with 6+ slices) or need to show precise differences — small slices are difficult to compare.

Scatter Plot
Tap to learn more ↓

Plots individual data points on two axes to reveal relationships between two variables. A pattern of dots trending up-right means a positive correlation.

✅ Use when

Investigating if two things are linked — e.g. does more sleep = better grades? Does temperature affect ice cream sales?

❌ Avoid when

You only have one variable or categorical data — a scatter plot needs two numerical values per data point.

Structured Data — Modelling Real Things

Computers use structured data to model real-world objects and events. Every row represents one real thing (like a student, a product, or a weather reading) and every column stores one type of information about it.

This structure lets computers sort, filter, search and compare data instantly — imagine trying to find the highest scorer in a class of 500 by reading through a paragraph about each student vs. scanning a column of numbers!

A well-structured dataset has: consistent column names, one data type per column, and no missing values.

Designing Algorithms

VCDTCD042 — Algorithms: Diagrammatic & English Design

What is an Algorithm?

An algorithm is a step-by-step set of instructions to solve a problem. Algorithms are the recipe that programmers turn into code.

Before writing any code, good programmers design their algorithm in plain language or as a flowchart. This helps find errors early!

Why Use a Flowchart?

Imagine trying to explain a complicated game to a friend using only words — it gets confusing fast. A flowchart turns your algorithm into a visual diagram, making it much easier to understand, check, and share.

🐛 Catch mistakes early

Drawing the steps out reveals logic errors before you write a single line of code — much easier to fix a diagram than broken code!

🤝 Share your thinking

A flowchart is a universal language — another programmer anywhere in the world can read your diagram and understand exactly how your program works.

🧩 Break big problems down

Complex programs can have hundreds of steps. A flowchart lets you map out each decision and action clearly, so nothing gets missed.

Why Do Flowcharts Use Specific Symbols?

Just like road signs have standard shapes (a triangle means warning, a circle means instruction), flowcharts use agreed symbols so that everyone reads them the same way. These symbols are used by programmers, engineers and designers worldwide.

⬭
Oval — Terminator
Marks the START or END of the algorithm. Every flowchart must have both.
▭
Rectangle — Process
An action the computer performs internally, e.g. "Add 1 to score" or "Sort the list".
◇
Diamond — Decision
A yes/no question that creates a branch, e.g. "Is score > 50?". Always has two exits: YES and NO.
▱
Parallelogram — Input / Output
Data coming in (user types something) or going out (displaying a result on screen).
→
Arrow — Flow
Connects shapes and shows the direction the algorithm moves — always follow the arrows!
💡 Think of it like this: the shape tells you what type of step it is, and the text inside tells you what specifically happens. Together they make the diagram self-explanatory to anyone who reads it.

Evaluate & Improve Solutions

VCDTCD044 — Evaluate Solutions: Needs, Innovation & Sustainability

Why Do We Evaluate?

Building something is only half the job. A good digital technologist asks: "Does this actually solve the problem? Could it be better? Will it still work in 5 years?"

When evaluating a digital solution, we look at three key areas:

  • Meeting needs — Does it actually do what users need?
  • Innovation — Is it a creative or clever approach?
  • Sustainability — Does it consider economic, environmental, and social impacts?

The Three Types of Sustainability

  • 🌱 Environmental — Does it use less energy? Does it reduce waste? (e.g. paperless systems)
  • 💰 Economic — Is it affordable to run long-term? Does it create or destroy jobs?
  • 🤝 Social — Is it accessible to everyone? Does it respect privacy and human rights?
